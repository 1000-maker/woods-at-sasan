About Us — Where the orchard meets the lion's range

The only place on earth the Asiatic lion still roams wild. A mango orchard on the edge of Gir Forest National Park, and within it, a retreat built on an older idea of hospitality — one that replenishes rather than consumes.

Woods at Sasan occupies eight acres on the boundary of Gir Forest National Park — the sole remaining habitat of the wild Asiatic lion. The property grows out of a working mango orchard, its architecture shaped by vernacular material: bela stone, lime plaster, recycled ship-teak, filtered light. More than four hundred trees were preserved during construction; 75.3% of the site remains a living landscape.

The retreat is the flagship of 1000 Island Hotels & Resorts, founded on a model of regenerative hospitality — the conviction that a stay should leave a place, and its community, more alive than it was found. That philosophy governs everything from the zero-cement build to the on-site medicinal forest that feeds the well-being programme directly.

Biophilia

The land came first. The architecture followed.

The term biophilia — popularised by biologist Edward O. Wilson in 1984 — describes something older than any word for it: our urge to affiliate with other forms of life. For most of human evolution, nature was not a backdrop. It was the condition of existence. We are only recently indoors.

At Woods at Sasan, biophilia is not a design principle layered onto a building. It is the starting point. The land came first. The architecture followed, orienting itself around what was already here — orchard, grassland, river, forest edge — and asking only what it needed to shelter, never what it wanted to dominate.

Biophilic Design Approach

Six ways the building defers to the land.

The design was shaped by what this landscape offered — a wildlife reserve, a working orchard, a river within walking distance — and by conscious decisions to maximise the guest's relationship with natural processes, not merely natural scenery.
01

Physical & visual connection

Full-size openings, fewer walls. Morning sun falls directly onto the bed. Guest rooms look out to the mango orchard on one side and the deciduous forest on the other, where a family of deer in the early morning is not unusual.

02

Sensory connection

Decks, outdoor bathing, the unpredicted sway of grasses. Stone walls and lime plaster; furniture in natural wood, cane, khadi, banana fibre. Materials that breathe, that respond to weather and time, as all living things do.

03

Experiences to observe nature

Forest walks with a local naturalist. The Som medicinal garden. Stargazing through the house telescope. A barbecue under the fire pit while crickets provide the score. Countless opportunities to engage with the abundance of this specific place.

04

Natural patterns in design

Like meandering trails formed by grazing animals, branched walkways connect the guest rooms to the rest of the property. Buildings are placed where the light falls — as a sapling grows — leaving the existing landscape untouched.

05

Crafts as ecological storytelling

Each handcrafted detail narrates the region — its flora, its fauna, its coexistences. Design here is not decoration. It is correspondence between the built space and the living world that surrounds it.

06

A cyclic system of inclusion

Rainwater, food waste, pruning and soil form a closed loop. Nothing is disposed of; everything is returned. The property does not merely sit within an ecosystem — it participates in one.

Water

Rainwater → groundwater → landscape

Natural terrain slows runoff. Water seeps into soil, raises the water table, is purified and returned to irrigate the orchard through summer.

Soil

Food waste → compost → harvest

Kitchen waste and pruned plantation go into the Bioneer composter. The resulting soil returns nutrients to the edible garden within 24 hours.

Ecology

Design → biodiversity

Native planting, preserved terrain and minimal intervention have created conditions in which over 50 bird species and 20 butterfly species now live on the property.

A handmade relief-work mural mapping the unique ecological elements of Gir — the species, the habitats, the relationships that hold the ecosystem together. Made by local hands from local earth.

Room panels drawn from local bird species — geometry and thread mimicking the patterns of feather, wing and habitat. The same craft that formed the Swadesh ceiling extends into the private rooms, present in quieter scale.

The mango leaf in carved wooden doors. A pixelated mango tree in corten steel. A mango orchard scene painted in clay. The orchard the property grows within finds its way into every surface — not as pattern, but as acknowledgement.

An exploration of three natural materials — wood, stone and cotton — tracing their journey from the earth and back to it. The first thing a guest encounters: a quiet argument for the interconnectedness of all things.
The Swadesh Wall — how it came to be

From body to wall, from needle to clay.

Two researchers from the property's design team travelled to a Maldhari village in Gir to learn Chundana from its source — sitting with the artisans, observing the hand-poked geometry, understanding the motifs not as pattern but as language. The lotus. The sacred dot. The forms that tribal communities have pressed into skin for centuries as marks of identity and spiritual protection. They returned with that vocabulary and transposed it — from body to wall, from needle to clay — in a Lippan relief installation that now lives in Swadesh. The mud is raised, the mirrors pressed in by hand, and the motifs are those of the Maldhari: unchanged in meaning, moved into a new material. It is one of the few places in the property where two folk traditions from the same landscape meet in a single surface.

Pustak Ghar (Community Library) — a house of books

Knowledge, placed where people already are.

Pustak Ghar is a social experiment in accessible knowledge — reading libraries placed not only throughout the resort, but across thirteen villages of Sasan. Handcrafted wooden shelves, shaped like small houses, hold over two thousand donated titles, open to anyone who passes.

The conviction is simple: information belongs to everyone, and a book left within reach of a child in a village does quiet, compounding work. The shelf is the gesture; the reading is the point.

Culture at Woods at Sasan

It is simply present.

Culture here is not performed for guests — it is simply present. In the ceiling above dinner, in an evening stroll, in the hands of a potter, in a dance that arrived on another continent's shores three hundred years ago.

Nail & Thread · Craft Installation

Madhuram & Sureshbhai

Artisans, Borvav village, Gir
Swadesh ceiling installation

The star-shaped ceiling in Swadesh — its geometry drawn from the lotus — was handcrafted by Madhuram, an artisan from Borvav village. The craft: hundreds of nails placed with millimetric precision, then thread looped and tightened until the pattern acquires depth and shadow. Sureshbhai, also of Borvav, continues the lineage in works drawn from mythology and sacred forms. No shortcuts; no mass production. Only the patience of hands that have done this ten thousand times.

Pottery · Earth & Form

Jethábhai

Potter, Gir region
Vessels throughout the property

Clay is one of the oldest conversations between human hands and the earth beneath them. Jethábhai's pottery carries the grammar of this landscape — its colour, its weight, its quality of having been made close to where it is used. Guests who find a vessel at the table or a form in a corner of the room are in the presence of that conversation.

Music · Flute & Bulbul Tara

Rajubhai

Musician, Gir region
Evening performances, orchard & property

The bulbul tara — a folk string instrument of this region — produces a sound that belongs precisely here: in evening air, beside the orchard, at the hour when the forest changes register. Rajubhai plays both flute and bulbul tara. His music does not accompany the property; it completes it. When he plays, the distinction between the built and the wild dissolves briefly, which is the whole point of being in Gir.

Community · Resident Ambassador

Kaki

Senior Islander
Woods at Sasan, since inception

Each evening, Kaki — the most senior member of the Woods at Sasan team — takes a stroll through the property. She stops to ask guests about their day, their safari, what they ate. At Terracotta she arrives with a pickle trolley: seasonal specialties and the unhurried authority of someone who has been here since the first day the doors opened. Her knowledge of Sasan is not trained — it is lived.

Dance · Living Heritage

Siddi Dhamal

Siddi community, Gir & Saurashtra
Spiritual dance form · 300-year lineage

Three hundred years ago, the ancestors of the Siddi community were brought to Gujarat by Portuguese trade routes and presented to the Nawab of Junagadh. What they carried — their faith, their percussion, their movement — has never stopped. The Dhamal, also known as Mashira, arrives as prayer and dance simultaneously: a spiritual offering to the Siddi saint, rooted in African devotional tradition and shaped by three centuries of life in this forest.

Ecology · Coexistence

The Maldharis of Gir

Pastoral community
Resident in Gir for generations

The Maldharis have lived alongside the Asiatic lion for generations. Their settlements — the ness — are not separate from the forest; they are part of its rhythm. Their practice: take only what is needed, adapt to seasonal movement, respect the boundaries of the wild. Not a sustainability principle — an inheritance. Gir is protected not only by policy, but by people who have always known how to belong within it.

How to Get to Sasan

Sasan is closer than it seems.

The forest sits at the end of a road easier to reach than its remoteness suggests. Three airports, one railway corridor and one long, unhurried drive — each a different way of arriving.
By Air
Keshod Airport
Closest · ~40 km · 45 min by road. Flights from Mumbai (alternate days). The quiet entry, preferred by those who know.
Rajkot International
Best-connected · ~160 km · 2.5–3 hrs. Daily flights from Mumbai, Delhi and Ahmedabad. The most reliable air option from across India.
Diu Airport
~95 km · ~1.5 hrs. Flights from Mumbai. A coastal approach, suited to combining Gir with a few days by the sea.
By Rail
Sasan Gir Station
On the Veraval–Junagadh metre-gauge line. The train arrives at the property's doorstep — unhurried, and quite particular. A few services daily connect to Junagadh and onward.
Veraval Railway Station
Nearest broad-gauge railhead · ~45 km. Connected to Mumbai (Somnath Express), Ahmedabad and Rajkot. A transfer onward takes under an hour.
Junagadh Junction
~65 km. Well-connected from Ahmedabad and Mumbai. A morning at Girnar before continuing to Gir makes Junagadh a considered entry point.
By Road
From Ahmedabad
~360 km · 5–5.5 hrs via NH-927. The longest drive, but a good one — the land flattens and opens as Saurashtra begins, and the last hour through Gir is the slowest and best.
From Rajkot
~160 km · 2.5 hrs. Straightforward, well-maintained roads through Junagadh. For guests combining Rajkot with Gir, this corridor works cleanly.
From Junagadh
~65 km · ~1.5 hrs. The most scenic approach — past Girnar, through the peripheral forest. Short enough to feel like an extension of the orchard itself.
On Site · Helicopter
Sasan Gir Heliport
For guests arriving by charter, the heliport sits directly adjacent to the retreat. Private transfers can be arranged from Ahmedabad, Mumbai or Rajkot.
